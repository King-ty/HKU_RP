\begin{center}
    \Large \textbf{Abstract}
\end{center}
\vspace{0.4cm}

The unprecedented growth in both parameter scale and context window size of Large Language Models (LLMs) has made multi-node distributed inference essential for modern deployment.
However, prevailing parallelism strategies employed in inference scenarios---including Tensor Parallelism (TP), Expert Parallelism (EP), and Context Parallelism (CP)---suffer from significant inefficiencies attributable to substantial inter-node communication overhead.
Although Pipeline Parallelism (PP) presents a promising approach to mitigate these communication bottlenecks, it frequently encounters severe load imbalance---manifesting as pipeline bubbles---due to the inherent heterogeneity and dynamic workloads of LLM inference.

This proposal presents \sys, a pipeline-balanced LLM inference system that systematically addresses three sources of imbalance in PP-based inference: \modelimb, \phaseimb, and \requestimb.
\sys introduces \centralmtp and \overheadpartition to address \modelimb, \slicedkv and \stagekv to address \phaseimb, and \timesched leveraging a quadratic cost model to address \requestimb.

Preliminary evaluations on a \clusterconfig running DeepSeek-R1 (671B) show that \sys improves end-to-end throughput by up to \vllmgain over vLLM-Ascend and by \omnigain over OmniInfer under the same SLO constraints, while keeping the pipeline bubble ratio below \bubblecap and preserving model accuracy.
These results indicate that pipeline-balanced inference is a viable path toward efficient serving of future trillion-parameter LLMs.

\vspace{0.8cm}
\noindent \textbf{Keywords:} Large language models, inference systems, pipeline parallelism, KV cache, scheduling.

\section{Introduction}

Large Language Models (LLMs) have emerged as a cornerstone of modern artificial intelligence~\cite{survey}.
The industry is witnessing a substantial capital reallocation, with compute budgets definitively shifting from model training to deployment, thereby rendering inference efficiency the new critical bottleneck~\cite{splitwise}.
This rapid evolution has been characterized by a massive expansion in both parameter scale (e.g., DeepSeek-R1 671B~\cite{deepseek-r1}, Kimi K2 1T~\cite{kimi2}) and context window sizes (e.g., Qwen3 1M tokens~\cite{qwen3}).

While unlocking unprecedented capabilities, this expansion imposes prohibitive memory constraints on the underlying serving infrastructure.
A single inference node (e.g., equipped with $8 \times$ H800 GPUs) often lacks sufficient High Bandwidth Memory (HBM) to host the full weights of massive models alongside the runtime \kvcache under large batch sizes.
Consequently, multi-node distributed inference has become an imperative necessity for deployment, shifting the system bottleneck from memory capacity to inter-node communication bandwidth.

However, commonly employed parallelism strategies in inference scenarios---such as Tensor Parallelism (TP)~\cite{megatron}, Expert Parallelism (EP)~\cite{deepseekv3,cloudmatrix384}, and Context Parallelism (CP)~\cite{ringattention,loongserve}---suffer from severe inefficiencies due to substantial inter-node communication overhead~\cite{splitwise,distserve}.
For instance, TP relies heavily on layer-wise All-Reduce collectives, which induce severe latency degradation across nodes~\cite{alpa}.

To mitigate inter-node communication bottlenecks, Pipeline Parallelism (PP)~\cite{gpipe,seesaw} emerges as an ideal candidate, as it requires only the transfer of a limited volume of hidden states between stages, resulting in orders-of-magnitude lower communication traffic than TP, CP, or EP (Table~\ref{tab:parallel_comm}).
However, while PP is widely adopted in large-scale training~\cite{gpipe,pipedream,terapipe,zerobubble,vocabparallel}, its application to online LLM inference remains challenging.
This is primarily because the inherent heterogeneity and workload dynamics characteristic of LLM inference frequently trigger various forms of load imbalance, manifesting as pipeline bubbles~\cite{gpipe}.
We systematically categorize these imbalances into three primary dimensions:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{\Modelimb.} Many contemporary LLMs exhibit heterogeneous workloads across layers (e.g., DeepSeek-R1 integrates both dense and MoE layers~\cite{deepseek-r1,deepseekv3}), where naive layer-wise partitioning leads to mismatched execution times across stages.
    Multi-token prediction (MTP)~\cite{eagle,deepseekv3} further exacerbates this imbalance: MTP modules share weights with the base model's Embedding and LM Head, so PP separates these shared components across stages (Embedding in Stage~0, LM Head in Stage~$N{-}1$), introducing additional cross-stage communication, inducing pipeline bubbles due to mutual dependencies between MTP and the base-model execution flow, and causing hardware idling on middle stages that cannot participate in MTP computation.

    \item \textbf{\Phaseimb.} LLM inference comprises two distinct phases with fundamentally different computational characteristics---\Prefillterm and \Decodeterm~\cite{splitwise,distserve}. Their large execution-time disparity readily leads to pipeline bubbles across batches~\cite{dejavu}.
    While Prefill-Decode (PD) disaggregation architectures~\cite{splitwise,distserve,mooncake,dejavu} mitigate cross-phase interference, they introduce new challenges in PP scenarios: many existing systems defer \kvcache transmission until the entire model completes to maximize bandwidth utilization, forcing early stages to accumulate KV caches of all in-flight micro-batches and thus stall the pipeline when early stages exhaust HBM due to bursty requests.
    Furthermore, PP fragments \kvcache across stages, transforming simple point-to-point transfers into complex stage-to-stage routing that significantly complicates \kvcache management.

    \item \textbf{\Requestimb.} In online inference, the extreme variance in request lengths naturally leads to highly inconsistent micro-batch execution times~\cite{sarathi}, which is a non-negligible source of pipeline bubbles.
    Existing schedulers (e.g., Sarathi-Serve~\cite{sarathi}) employ token-budget-based heuristics, but these methods assume a linear relationship between execution time and token count, fundamentally overlooking the quadratic complexity of attention mechanisms~\cite{attention-complexity}.
    Since subsequent chunks must attend to the \kvcache of all preceding chunks, the computational overhead grows progressively, causing token-based scheduling to incur severe pipeline bubbles for long-context requests.
\end{itemize}

To address these imbalance challenges, we propose \sys, a pipeline-balanced high-performance inference system featuring a synergistic co-design of pipeline parallelism with complementary inference techniques.
To resolve \modelimb, we integrate PP with MTP by introducing \centralmtp and \overheadpartition, which consolidate MTP computation into the final stage and balance stage-wise workloads through execution-cost modeling.
To address \phaseimb, we unify PP with PD disaggregation by establishing connections based on PP stages to facilitate \slicedkv and \stagekv, effectively preventing \kvcache accumulation in the \prefillterm stage.
Finally, to address \requestimb, we design a \timesched algorithm that balances micro-batch workloads by explicitly modeling their execution time rather than relying solely on token counts.

Preliminary evaluations on a \clusterconfig running DeepSeek-R1 (671B) demonstrate \sys's superior performance.
Under strict Service Level Objectives (SLOs), \sys enhances end-to-end throughput by up to \vllmgain compared to the open-source vLLM-Ascend standard, and outperforms the highly optimized OmniInfer industrial baseline by \omnigain.
Crucially, it strictly bounds pipeline bubbles to under \bubblecap while maintaining zero accuracy degradation.

Our main contributions are as follows:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item We systematically characterize three types of load imbalance in PP-based inference: \modelimb, \phaseimb, and \requestimb.
    \item We propose \sys, an LLM inference system that achieves holistic balance through a synergistic co-design of pipeline parallelism with complementary inference techniques and a computation-aware scheduler.
    \item We provide preliminary evidence on a production-scale NPU setting that balanced PP can significantly improve inference throughput while preserving correctness.
\end{itemize}

\section{Motivation}
\label{sec:moti}

In this section, we analyze why Pipeline Parallelism represents the most viable strategy for deploying massive models such as DeepSeek-R1 in cross-node scenarios and expose the fundamental causes of pipeline bubbles.

\subsection{The Opportunity for Pipeline Parallelism}

The evolution of Large Language Models has demonstrated the power of scaling laws~\cite{scaling-laws}: increasing model parameters enhances foundational capabilities, while extending inference-time computation improves complex reasoning~\cite{test-time-compute}.
However, the massive model size and the memory footprint of \kvcache for long contexts exert immense pressure on accelerator memory, frequently exceeding the capacity of a single computing node.
For instance, a DeepSeek-R1 model~\cite{deepseek-r1} with 671B parameters surpasses the 640\,GB memory limit of a standard H800 node ($8 \times 80$\,GB).
Consequently, multi-node deployment has become indispensable.

Among existing parallelism strategies, Data Parallelism (DP) scales throughput by replicating models but fails to address memory constraints, as it does not partition the model.
Context Parallelism (CP), such as Ring Attention~\cite{ringattention} and LoongServe~\cite{loongserve}, handles long sequences by partitioning context but remains ineffective against massive weights and incurs high communication overhead by passing \kvcache layer by layer.
Tensor Parallelism (TP)~\cite{megatron} achieves intra-layer parallelism but requires heavy layer-wise All-Reduce communication, rendering it unsuitable for inter-node scenarios with limited bandwidth.
Moreover, for deep and narrow models with smaller hidden sizes, excessive TP partitions matrices too finely, leading to degraded computation efficiency and cache hit rates.
TP also interacts poorly with \mla architectures~\cite{deepseekv2}: because MLA compresses the \kvcache into a low-rank latent space that cannot be partitioned across TP ranks, every rank must redundantly store the full latent \kvcache, negating much of TP's memory-saving benefit.
For MoE models such as DeepSeek-R1, Expert Parallelism (EP)~\cite{deepseekv3,cloudmatrix384} is effective but suffers from high layer-wise All-to-All communication costs and potential bubbles due to expert load imbalance.

As shown in Table~\ref{tab:parallel_comm}, Pipeline Parallelism transmits only a small volume of activations between stages, resulting in significantly lower communication traffic than alternative strategies.
While industry systems often employ engineering techniques such as double-batching to hide communication costs, the inherent dependency between computation and communication for a single request implies that inference latency remains bottlenecked by slow inter-node links.
Therefore, PP is a very promising optimization candidate because it fundamentally reduces cross-node communication overhead.

However, while PP offers a pathway to low-bandwidth multi-node inference, the pipeline imbalance bubble issue remains the primary obstacle that must be addressed.

\subsection{Sources of Pipeline Imbalance}

While Pipeline Parallelism offers significant potential for cross-node deployment of large-scale models, its performance is often hindered by pipeline bubbles.
Pipeline bubbles can be categorized into two types: \textbf{inherent bubbles} and \textbf{imbalance bubbles}, as shown in Figure~\ref{fig:2bubbetypes}.

\begin{figure}[!htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.75\textwidth]{pics/2-bubble-types.pdf}
    \caption{Two pipeline bubble types: \textbf{inherent bubbles} arise during warm-up and cool-down, while \textbf{imbalance bubbles} persist throughout steady-state execution.}
    \label{fig:2bubbetypes}
\end{figure}

Inherent bubbles arise from the intrinsic data dependencies during pipeline warm-up and cool-down phases.
During warm-up, early stages process the first micro-batch while later stages remain idle; during cool-down, later stages process the final micro-batch while earlier stages have completed.
These inherent bubbles can be amortized and safely ignored over long generation periods when the number of micro-batches significantly exceeds the number of pipeline stages ($N \gg P$)~\cite{gpipe}.

In contrast, \textbf{imbalance bubbles} persist throughout the entire pipeline execution, continuously degrading accelerator utilization and must be systematically mitigated.
These imbalance bubbles stem from three fundamental causes: (1)~\textit{uneven stage partitioning}, where different stages exhibit mismatched execution times; (2)~\textit{inconsistent micro-batch workloads}, where different micro-batches require varying processing times; and (3)~\textit{data dependencies}, where stages must wait for data from other stages before proceeding.
Figure~\ref{fig:imbalance} illustrates imbalance bubbles caused by uneven stage partitioning and inconsistent micro-batch workloads, with cross-stage data dependencies implicitly underlying both scenarios.
In the context of modern LLM inference, we identify three specific types of imbalance bubbles arising from these fundamental causes: \textbf{\modelimb}, \textbf{\phaseimb}, and \textbf{\requestimb}.

\begin{figure}[!htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.82\textwidth]{pics/imbalance-stage-batch.pdf}
    \caption{Three root causes of imbalance bubbles: uneven stage partitioning, inconsistent micro-batch workloads, and cross-stage data dependencies.}
    \label{fig:imbalance}
\end{figure}

\subsubsection{\Modelimb}

Modern LLMs exhibit heterogeneous architectural characteristics that invalidate naive layer-wise partitioning strategies.
Contemporary LLMs integrate Dense and Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) FFNs~\cite{deepseekv3}, as well as Full and Linear Attention mechanisms across different layers.
Naive partitioning by layer count inevitably leads to severe workload imbalances across stages, as different layer types exhibit vastly different computational costs.

A more significant challenge arises from MTP~\cite{eagle,deepseekv3}, as shown in Figure~\ref{fig:mtp-integration}.
MTP is a training-inference co-designed speculative decoding paradigm that is becoming the new industry standard for high-throughput inference.
MTP modules deeply share weights with the base model's Embedding and LM Head.
Recent PP studies likewise show that output-side post-processing or vocabulary-related computation can distort stage balance~\cite{vocabparallel,dynapipe}, but MTP introduces an additional cross-stage dependency between the shared Embedding and LM Head.
In naive PP, placing the Embedding at Stage~0 and the LM Head at Stage~$N{-}1$ forces intermediate activations to ``round-trip'' across the cluster, incurring additional inter-stage communication.
This mutual dependency also stalls intermediate stages during MTP computation, introducing structural imbalance bubbles as these stages remain idle.

Figure~\ref{fig:mtp-integration} compares the naive and proposed designs using a consistent three-stage pipeline.
The naive design places the Embedding layer at Stage~0 and the LM Head at Stage~2, forcing MTP-related information to cross the pipeline boundary again.
In \sys, Stage~0 retains the normal Embedding layer for base-model execution, while Stage~2 redundantly loads an Embedding copy together with MTP and the LM Head.
This removes MTP round-trip communication and leaves only the ordinary forward data flow.

\begin{figure}[!htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{pics/mtp-solu.pdf}
    \caption{MTP integration under pipeline parallelism. The naive design (left) introduces extra communication and idle stages, whereas \sys's centralized design (right) consolidates all MTP computation into the final stage, eliminating cross-stage MTP communication.}
    \label{fig:mtp-integration}
\end{figure}

\subsubsection{\Phaseimb}

LLM inference comprises two distinct phases with fundamentally different computational characteristics: the \Prefillterm phase, which processes the prompt in parallel to generate the first output token, exhibiting compute-bound behavior; and the \Decodeterm phase, which iteratively generates subsequent tokens one by one, exhibiting memory-bound behavior~\cite{splitwise,distserve}.
This inherent dichotomy creates interference when both phases are co-located.

A single \prefillterm step often takes substantially longer than a \decodeterm step, causing prefill-dominant micro-batches to run significantly slower than decode-dominant micro-batches and drastically degrading Time Between Tokens (TBT).
This drastic time discrepancy creates highly variable micro-batch execution times, thereby generating imbalance bubbles.

While disaggregated Prefill-Decode (PD) architectures~\cite{splitwise,distserve,dejavu,mooncake,tetriinfer,memserve,pdserve,flowkv} have become the standard approach to mitigate cross-phase interference, they introduce new challenges in PP scenarios.
Existing systems widely employ bulk transmission, deferring \kvcache transfer until the entire \Prefillterm phase completes to maximize bandwidth utilization.
However, under Pipeline Parallelism, this mechanism forces early stages (e.g., Stage~0) to passively wait for all subsequent stages to finish before they can transmit and release their local \kvcache.
As illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:kv-accumulation}, under bursty traffic conditions, large micro-batches exhaust early-stage memory resources.
Since the \kvcache is held until the end of the pipeline, subsequent micro-batches are starved of resources, forcing the whole pipeline to stall and creating massive bubbles.

Furthermore, PP inherently fragments the \kvcache across stages, transforming traditional simple point-to-point communication into complex stage-to-stage routing.
Each \Prefillterm stage generates only its assigned layers' \kvcache slice, and each \Decodeterm stage requires only the slice corresponding to its assigned layers.
This many-to-many routing pattern poses significant management challenges and threatens system robustness.

    \label{fig:kv-accumulation}
\end{figure}

\subsubsection{\Requestimb}

The substantial variance in request lengths makes it difficult to align micro-batch execution times, thereby introducing further pipeline bubbles.
Existing schedulers (e.g., Sarathi-Serve~\cite{sarathi} and gLLM~\cite{gllm}) employ chunking- or token-budget-based strategies to mitigate this by chunking long requests into smaller pieces and limiting the total token count per micro-batch.
However, such token-budget-based methods assume a linear relationship between execution time and token count, fundamentally overlooking the quadratic complexity of attention mechanisms.

As sequence length increases, quadratic attention computation becomes dominant.
Since subsequent chunks must attend to the \kvcache of all preceding chunks, the computational overhead grows progressively~\cite{attention-complexity}.
This progressive increase in chunk execution time causes token-based scheduling to suffer from severe pipeline bubbles when handling long-context requests, as shown in Figure~\ref{fig:sarathi-bubble}.

Existing solutions face significant limitations.
Dynamic programming approaches~\cite{terapipe} can theoretically optimize chunk sizes for balanced execution times, but they incur high computational complexity ($O(mnl^2)$) and are difficult to apply to varying request lengths in real-time inference scenarios.
FLOPs-based empirical formulas attempt to estimate execution time through computational cost modeling, but they account only for theoretical computation while overlooking memory access and communication overhead.
Furthermore, these formulas rely on theoretical FLOPs values, which may deviate significantly from actual execution costs due to varying operator implementations, such as FlashAttention~\cite{flashattention}.

\section{Research Methodology}

In this section, we present the system design of \sys.
We first provide an overview of the system architecture, followed by an in-depth description of the three core techniques developed to address the multi-dimensional load imbalance in pipeline-parallel LLM inference.

\subsection{System Overview}

As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:overview}, \sys adopts a disaggregated Prefill-Decode architecture, partitioning the compute cluster into separate \Prefillterm and \Decodeterm pools, each potentially configured with different pipeline parallelism strategies.
Both \Prefillterm and \Decodeterm instances are equipped with MTP modules: the \Prefillterm instance's MTP module is invoked once per request to generate the MTP \kvcache, while the \Decodeterm instance's MTP module is invoked multiple times per generation step to produce multiple candidate tokens.
To ensure pipeline efficiency, \sys implements \Centralmtp and \Overheadpartition (Section~\ref{sec:partition}) to address \modelimb by consolidating MTP computation into the final stage and balancing stage-wise workloads.
Between \Prefillterm and \Decodeterm nodes, \sys establishes stage-to-stage \kvcache transfer connections, managed by dedicated \kvconnector{}s that enable \Slicedkv and \Stagekv (Section~\ref{sec:kvcache}) to resolve \phaseimb through fine-grained, on-demand transmission.
Finally, \sys incorporates \Timesched (Section~\ref{sec:scheduling}) to address \requestimb, which leverages a quadratic cost model to maintain balanced micro-batch execution times even under highly variable request lengths.

\begin{figure}[!htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{pics/overview.pdf}
    \caption{System overview of \sys. \sys combines disaggregated \Prefillterm-\Decodeterm clusters, \centralmtp, stage-wise \kvcache transfer, and \timesched to address all three dimensions of pipeline imbalance.}
    \label{fig:overview}
\end{figure}

\subsection{\Centralmtp and \Overheadpartition}
\label{sec:partition}

A key challenge in integrating MTP with PP lies in the \modelimb.
As analyzed in Section~\ref{sec:moti}, MTP introduces long-range dependencies between the shared Embedding (Stage~0) and LM Head (Stage~$N{-}1$), which directly conflicts with the locality assumption inherent in pipeline parallelism.
To resolve this, we propose a two-pronged approach that achieves structural and temporal balance through \Centralmtp and \Overheadpartition.

\subsubsection{\Centralmtp}

To eliminate the structural conflict caused by dispersed MTP components, we implement \textit{weight relocalization}.
By redundantly loading the Embedding layer weights into the final pipeline stage (Stage~$N{-}1$), we transform logic-level global dependencies into physical-level local computation.

This architectural decision offers two deterministic advantages.
First, it restores pipeline locality by completely eliminating the ``round-trip'' inter-stage communication during speculative drafting.
Second, it ensures that all intermediate stages can participate in the base-model execution flow without being stalled by MTP-related data dependencies, effectively removing structural pipeline bubbles.

The tradeoff is that the final stage now carries additional computation and memory overhead, which motivates the partitioning strategy below.

\subsubsection{\Overheadpartition}

While the centralized design restores locality, it shifts the system bottleneck, rendering Stage~$N{-}1$ a potential computation hotspot that must handle both base-model layers and multi-round MTP invocations.
To equalize the stage-wise duty cycles, we propose an Overhead-Aware Balanced Partitioning algorithm.

Unlike existing PP partitioning methods that treat MTP as a single monolithic layer, our approach explicitly models the recurrent execution overhead of MTP modules as a function of the speculation depth.
We profile the per-layer execution costs through offline benchmarking and formulate the partitioning as a discrete optimization problem.
To preserve the arithmetic intensity and minimize communication overhead, partition points are strictly restricted to residual connection boundaries.

The optimization objective is to minimize the total execution time for $N$ micro-batches:
\begin{equation}
T = \sum_{i=1}^{P} T_i + (N-1) \cdot \max_{1 \leq i \leq P} T_i,
\end{equation}
where $P$ is the number of pipeline stages and $T_i$ is the execution time of stage $i$.
Since $\sum_{i=1}^{P} T_i$ is fixed for a given micro-batch (the total per-layer cost is invariant to how layers are partitioned), the objective reduces to minimizing the bottleneck stage latency $T_{\max} = \max_i T_i$.
\sys solves this discrete partitioning problem efficiently in $O(L \log T)$ time via binary search on $T_{\max}$ with greedy verification, where $L$ is the total number of layers.
This approach is significantly more efficient than the dynamic programming used in Alpa~\cite{alpa}, as it reduces the search space to layer-level partition boundaries, bypassing exhaustive joint-configuration enumeration.

Through this overhead-aware strategy, Stage~$N{-}1$ is assigned fewer base-model layers to compensate for its MTP duties.
Crucially, this computation-memory tradeoff is self-correcting: the reduction in base-layer weights naturally offsets the HBM footprint of the redundantly loaded Embedding, achieving holistic resource equilibrium.

\subsection{\Slicedkv and \Stagekv}
\label{sec:kvcache}

To address \phaseimb, we propose a sliced \kvcache management strategy with stage-wise KV transmission.
The key insight is that PP induces a fixed, layer-wise partitioning of \kvcache across stages: while the execution time of \Prefillterm and \Decodeterm phases fluctuates, the spatial ownership and unidirectional dependency of each \kvcache slice remain invariant.
By localizing \kvcache management to each stage, \sys decouples each stage's data transfer from the global pipeline, enabling early release of KV memory and eliminating the accumulation bottleneck of bulk transmission.

\subsubsection{\Slicedkv}

In PP-based PD disaggregation, each \Prefillterm stage generates only a layer-wise slice of the complete \kvcache, and each \Decodeterm stage requires only the slice corresponding to its assigned layers.
This transforms the simple point-to-point \kvcache transfer into a complex stage-to-stage routing problem.
To avoid the coordination overhead of a centralized manager, \sys treats each pipeline stage as an autonomous memory domain.

\sys addresses this by assigning a dedicated \kvconnector to each pipeline stage rather than using a monolithic connector for the entire cluster.
Each stage's connector independently manages its \kvcache slice, including tracking memory allocation, transfer status, and garbage collection.
This fine-grained management allows each prefill stage to reclaim memory immediately after its local slice is transmitted, maximizing HBM availability during bursty traffic.

\subsubsection{\Stagekv}

The conventional approach of deferring \kvcache transmission until all \Prefillterm stages complete causes memory accumulation and potential pipeline stalls.
This bulk-synchronization approach forces early stages to hold stale KV data until the entire pipeline reaches the cool-down phase.
\sys leverages the strictly unidirectional data dependency of \kvcache generation to enable stage-wise KV transmission, as shown in Figure~\ref{fig:stage-wise-kv}.

The transmission follows a pull-based model:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
    \item Guided by the pre-defined stage-to-layer mapping, each \Decodeterm stage's \kvconnector computes which \Prefillterm stages it needs to request \kvcache from.
    \item When a \Decodeterm stage is ready to receive \kvcache, it simultaneously sends pull requests to all connected \Prefillterm stages.
    \item Each \Prefillterm stage, upon completing its computation for a micro-batch, can immediately respond to \kvcache pull requests from \Decodeterm stages, transferring and freeing its local \kvcache slice without waiting for downstream stages.
\end{enumerate}

By shifting from pipeline-level synchronization to stage-level granularity, this design effectively prevents the early-stage memory exhaustion discussed in Section~\ref{sec:moti}.
It ensures that the \kvcache transmission flow is pipelined alongside the computation, thereby balancing bandwidth utilization and reducing the overall \kvcache transfer latency.

    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{pics/stage-wise-kv.pdf}
    \caption{\Slicedkv and \stagekv. Each stage independently manages and transfers its own \kvcache slice, eliminating the accumulation bottleneck of conventional bulk transmission.}
    \label{fig:stage-wise-kv}
\end{figure}

\subsection{\Timesched}
\label{sec:scheduling}

To address \requestimb, which arises from inherent request-length variance that the linear time-token assumption cannot adequately capture, we replace static token budgeting with fine-grained time budgeting.
By explicitly accounting for the non-linear execution cost of attention mechanisms, \Timesched transforms the scheduling problem from a heuristic-based token filling into a deterministic execution-time optimization.

\subsubsection{Quadratic Polynomial Cost Model}

We model the execution time of a micro-batch as a quadratic function of chunk length ($x$) and historical context length ($y$):
\begin{equation}
T(x,y) = a x^2 + bxy + cx + dy + e.
\end{equation}

This formulation provides a high-fidelity abstraction of the quadratic attention complexity while remaining computationally lightweight for real-time scheduling.
The terms in the model map to specific architectural operations:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item The $x^2$ term captures the quadratic self-attention within the current chunk.
    \item The $xy$ term captures the cross-attention between the current chunk and historical context.
    \item The $x$ term captures linear operations within the current chunk, such as linear projection and element-wise activation.
    \item The $y$ term captures linear operations on historical context, such as \kvcache memory access and MLA projection.
    \item The constant term $e$ encapsulates fixed system overheads, such as kernel launch latency and operator dispatch, ensuring accuracy even for small micro-batches.
\end{itemize}

To maintain the prediction accuracy of the cost model across diverse model architectures and operator implementations (e.g., FlashAttention~\cite{flashattention} vs.\ Standard Attention), the coefficients $(a,b,c,d,e)$ are initialized through a startup profiling run and can be continuously refined during runtime via Recursive Least Squares (RLS)~\cite{haykin}.
This closed-loop adaptation allows \sys to automatically compensate for hardware-level timing variances without manual re-tuning.
Each RLS update requires only $O(k^2)$ computation where $k{=}5$ is the number of coefficients, incurring negligible overhead.

\subsubsection{Time-Budget Scheduling Algorithm}

\Timesched achieves pipeline balance by enforcing a strict temporal constraint, $T_{\mathrm{budget}}$, on each micro-batch, ensuring uniform execution times across stages regardless of the non-linear attention cost.
Algorithm~\ref{alg:scheduling} presents the scheduling procedure.
Unlike traditional token-budget schedulers that assume a linear time-token relationship, \sys directly constrains execution time: when a request's estimated cost exceeds the remaining budget, the scheduler reverse-calculates the maximum allowable chunk size $x_i$ by solving the quadratic inequality $T_{\mathrm{est}} \le B$.

\begin{algorithm}[!htbp]
\caption{Computation-Aware Scheduling with Time-Budget Constraint}
\label{alg:scheduling}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Request queue $\mathcal{Q}$; time budget $T_{\mathrm{budget}}$; cost-model coefficients $\Theta = (a, b, c, d, e)$ initialized via offline profiling
\While{true} \Comment{Each iteration is one scheduling step}
    \State $\mathcal{M} \leftarrow \emptyset$
    \State $B \leftarrow T_{\mathrm{budget}}$
    \While{$\mathcal{Q}$ is not empty \textbf{and} $B > 0$}
        \State Pop request $r_i$ with remaining length $l_i$ and context length $y_i$
        \State $T_{\mathrm{est}} \leftarrow a l_i^2 + b l_i y_i + c l_i + d y_i + e$
        \If{$T_{\mathrm{est}} \le B$}
            \State $x_i \leftarrow l_i$
            \State $B \leftarrow B - T_{\mathrm{est}}$
        \Else \Comment{Reverse-calculate max chunk size}
            \State $\Delta \leftarrow (b y_i + c)^2 - 4a(d y_i + e - B)$
            \State $x_i \leftarrow \left\lfloor \frac{-(b y_i + c) + \sqrt{\Delta}}{2a} \right\rfloor$
            \State Update $r_i$'s remaining length to $l_i - x_i$; push $r_i$ back to $\mathcal{Q}$
            \State $B \leftarrow 0$
        \EndIf
        \State $\mathcal{M} \leftarrow \mathcal{M} \cup \{ \text{chunk}(r_i, x_i) \}$
    \EndWhile
    \State Execute micro-batch $\mathcal{M}$ on pipeline
    \If{online adaptation is enabled}
        \State Record actual latency $T_{\mathrm{actual}}$
        \State Update $\Theta$ via RLS with $(\mathcal{M}, T_{\mathrm{actual}})$
    \EndIf
\EndWhile
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

The time budget hyperparameter $T_{\mathrm{budget}}$ is empirically set to the execution time of a zero-context request that saturates the available HBM capacity, which effectively upper-bounds micro-batch cost and avoids out-of-memory failures under normal operating conditions.

By explicitly modeling execution time rather than relying on token counts, \sys achieves balanced micro-batch execution times even under highly variable request lengths, minimizing the accumulated pipeline bubbles that typically plague long-context inference.

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]

    \item \textbf{OmniInfer~\cite{omniinfer}:} a state-of-the-art industrial solution that implements MTP and PD disaggregation but without PP integration.

Unless otherwise stated, all systems are evaluated under the same 32-NPU hardware budget, the same DeepSeek-R1 checkpoint, the same MTP depth, the same batching-related limits, and the same \Prefillterm/\Decodeterm resource split.

\textbf{Workloads and metrics.}
We evaluated under four workload configurations: short-context fixed (2K), short-context variable (2K), long-context fixed (32K), and long-context variable (32K).
The fixed-length workloads serve as controlled stress tests in which all requests share the same context budget.
The variable-length workloads use synthetic traces with naturally varying prompt and generation lengths, including long-tail requests that stress the scheduler's ability to balance micro-batches.
The primary metric is end-to-end throughput under strict latency SLO constraints.
We also measure pipeline bubble ratio and verify model correctness on HumanEval~\cite{human-eval}.

\textbf{Research questions.}
The preliminary evaluation answers three questions:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{RQ1 (Throughput):} How much does \sys improve end-to-end throughput under identical SLOs?

\section{Related Work}

\paragraph{Pipeline parallelism and its optimization.}
Tensor parallelism~\cite{megatron}, expert parallelism~\cite{deepseekv3,cloudmatrix384}, and context parallelism~\cite{ringattention,loongserve} partition models along different dimensions but all introduce heavy per-layer cross-node communication (All-Reduce, All-to-All, or KV Cache).
Pipeline parallelism~\cite{gpipe} transfers only stage-boundary hidden states, making it attractive when inter-node bandwidth is limited.
Training-era PP scheduling---from GPipe's synchronous micro-batching~\cite{gpipe} through PipeDream's relaxed synchronization~\cite{pipedream}, TeraPipe's DP-based chunking~\cite{terapipe}, and Zero-Bubble's split-backward interleaving~\cite{zerobubble}---assumes homogeneous, fixed-size micro-batches, an assumption that breaks down in inference.
Inference-oriented works revisit PP with dynamic re-sharding (Seesaw~\cite{seesaw}), token throttling (gLLM~\cite{gllm}), temporal phase disaggregation (TD-Pipe~\cite{tdpipe}), layer redistribution (DynaPipe~\cite{dynapipe}), and CPU/GPU cooperation (SiPipe~\cite{sipipe}), but none jointly addresses the three categories of imbalance bubbles.

\paragraph{Serving runtimes and scheduling.}
Modern serving systems~\cite{orca,vllm,sarathi,fastserve,llumnix,sglang} have advanced scheduling and memory management through continuous batching, paged \kvcache, and chunked prefill, but their schedulers assume a linear relationship between token count and execution time.

\paragraph{Prefill-Decode disaggregation and KV cache management.}
Splitwise~\cite{splitwise} and DistServe~\cite{distserve} proposed PD disaggregation to isolate the compute-bound \Prefillterm from the memory-bound \Decodeterm phase.
Subsequent work extended this paradigm along multiple dimensions~\cite{dejavu,mooncake,memserve,pdserve,tetriinfer,flowkv,infinigen,cloudmatrix384}.
However, none of these systems address the challenges introduced when PD disaggregation is combined with PP: \Prefillterm-side \kvcache accumulation caused by deferred bulk transmission, and the complex stage-to-stage routing required for stage-partitioned \kvcache transfer.
\sys addresses both challenges through \slicedkv and \stagekv, which strikes a balance between bulk and layer-wise transfer by transmitting at stage granularity.

\paragraph{Speculative decoding and MTP-aware inference.}
Speculative decoding accelerates auto-regressive generation by drafting multiple candidate tokens and verifying them in a single forward pass~\cite{eagle,medusa,specinfer}.
Multi-Token Prediction (MTP)~\cite{mtp,deepseekv3,eagle} co-designs the training objective with inference-time speculation, and production systems~\cite{omniinfer,cloudmatrix384} have demonstrated substantial throughput gains from MTP integration.
However, prior work universally treats MTP as a decoding-only acceleration technique without considering its interaction with PP.
Once PP is introduced, MTP becomes a stage-placement and synchronization problem: the shared Embedding and LM Head weights are dispersed across pipeline stages, creating additional communication paths and idle stages.
\sys addresses this gap by consolidating all MTP computation into a single stage and incorporating multi-round MTP overhead into the partitioning objective.

% 6 Future Work
% ==========================================
\section{Future Work}

The preliminary evaluation suggests that balanced PP is promising for large-scale LLM serving, but several directions remain open.

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{Robustness and fault recovery.}
    A PP deployment is sensitive to stage failures because a single failed stage can block the entire pipeline.
    Future work will investigate lightweight recovery mechanisms, such as stage-level checkpointing or rapid stage migration, that use stage-wise KV metadata to recreate failed stages without recomputing from scratch.

    \item \textbf{Offline batch inference.}
    \sys currently targets online serving with strict latency SLOs.
    Adapting its scheduling and partitioning strategies to offline batch-inference scenarios---where throughput maximization without latency constraints is the primary goal---could unlock additional efficiency gains.

    \item \textbf{Joint time-memory budget optimization.}
    The current \timesched constrains micro-batch execution time rather than token count, which may leave a small amount of accelerator memory temporarily underutilized when the time budget is reached before memory capacity is fully occupied.
    Extending the scheduler to co-optimize time and memory budgets is a natural next step.
\end{itemize}

\section{Conclusion}

We systematically identified that naive pipeline parallelism in LLM inference fails because of multi-dimensional load imbalance: \modelimb, \phaseimb, and \requestimb.
We designed \sys, a pipeline-balanced high-performance LLM inference system that addresses all three imbalance dimensions through a synergistic co-design of \centralmtp, \overheadpartition, \slicedkv, \stagekv, and \timesched.
Preliminary evaluation on DeepSeek-R1 (671B) over Ascend 910C clusters demonstrates that \sys delivers up to \vllmgain throughput gains over open-source standards under the same SLO constraints, while strictly bounding pipeline bubbles below \bubblecap and maintaining zero accuracy degradation.
These results motivate continued work toward a robust, adaptive, and production-ready pipeline-balanced inference infrastructure for future trillion-parameter LLMs.
