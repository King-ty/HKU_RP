\begin{center}
    \Large \textbf{Abstract}
\end{center}
\vspace{0.5cm}
\setstretch{1.15}

The massive expansion of Large Language Models (LLMs) in both parameter scale and context window sizes has made multi-node distributed inference a strict necessity.
However, commonly used parallelism strategies in inference scenarios---such as Tensor Parallelism (TP), Expert Parallelism (EP), and Context Parallelism (CP)---suffer from severe inefficiencies due to high inter-node communication overhead.
Although Pipeline Parallelism (PP) seems to be an ideal candidate to mitigate these communication bottlenecks, it frequently encounters severe load imbalances---which manifest as notorious pipeline bubbles---due to the inherent heterogeneities and dynamic workloads of LLM inference scenarios.
In this work, we systematically characterize three primary load imbalance challenges in PP-based inference: Model-Partitioning Imbalance, Phase-Interference Imbalance, and Request-Variability Imbalance.

To mitigate these issues, we present \sys, a novel pipeline-balanced inference system designed to bridge the efficiency gap through a synergistic co-design of pipeline parallelism with other state-of-the-art inference techniques.
Specifically, \sys introduces: (1) Centralized Multi-Token Prediction (MTP) Processing and Overhead-Aware Partitioning to resolve Model-Partitioning Imbalance; (2) Sliced KV Cache Management and Stage-Wise KV Transmission to eliminate Phase-Interference Imbalance; and (3) Computation-Aware Scheduling leveraging a quadratic cost model to handle Request-Variability Imbalance.

Preliminary evaluations on a 32-NPU Ascend 910C cluster with DeepSeek-R1 (671B) demonstrate that \sys achieves up to 90.8\% end-to-end throughput improvement over state-of-the-art baselines like vLLM-Ascend under the same SLAs, while strictly bounding pipeline bubbles under 5\% with zero accuracy degradation.

\vspace{1cm}
\noindent \textbf{Key words:} Large Language Models (LLMs), Inference System, Pipeline Parallelism (PP), Load Balancing.

\section{Introduction}

In recent years, Large Language Models (LLMs) have emerged as the cornerstone of modern artificial intelligence~\cite{survey}.
The industry is witnessing a massive capital reallocation, with compute budgets definitively shifting from model training to deployment, making inference efficiency the new critical bottleneck~\cite{}.
To achieve this rapid evolution, state-of-the-art LLMs have undergone a massive expansion in both parameter scale (e.g., DeepSeek-R1 671B~\cite{deepseekv3}, Kimi-K2 1T~\cite{kimi2}) and context window sizes (e.g., Qwen3 1M tokens~\cite{qwen3}).
While unlocking unprecedented capabilities, this expansion imposes prohibitive memory constraints on the underlying serving infrastructure.
A single inference node (e.g., equipped with $8 \times$ H800 GPUs) often lacks sufficient High Bandwidth Memory (HBM) to host the full weights of massive models alongside the runtime KV cache under large batch sizes.
Consequently, multi-node distributed inference has become a necessity for deployment, shifting the system bottleneck from memory capacity to inter-node communication bandwidth.
However, commonly used parallelism strategies in inference scenarios---such as Tensor Parallelism (TP)~\cite{megatron}, Expert Parallelism (EP)~\cite{deepseekv3}, and Context Parallelism (CP)~\cite{ringattention}---suffer from severe inefficiencies due to high inter-node communication overhead~\cite{}.
For instance, TP relies heavily on layer-wise, high-bandwidth-consuming All-Reduce collectives, which causes severe latency degradation across nodes~\cite{alpa}.
Furthermore, for ``deep and thin'' models like DeepSeek-R1, TP fragments the already narrow hidden dimensions too finely, leading to insufficient computation grain size and diminished hardware utilization~\cite{}.

To mitigate inter-node communication bottlenecks, Pipeline Parallelism (PP)~\cite{gpipe} emerges as an ideal candidate as it only requires the transfer of a limited volume of activations between stages.
Compared to TP, PP also allows each layer to maintain a more complete computation shape, sustaining higher arithmetic intensity and cache locality in ``deep and thin'' models.
However, while PP is widely adopted in large-scale training, it remains challenging to apply in inference scenarios.
This is primarily because the \textbf{inherent heterogeneities and dynamic workloads} of LLM inference frequently trigger various forms of load imbalance, which manifest as notorious pipeline bubbles~\cite{}.
We systematically categorize these imbalances into three primary dimensions:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Model-Partitioning Imbalance:} Many contemporary LLMs exhibit heterogeneous workloads across layers (e.g., DeepSeek-R1 mixes dense and MoE layers~\cite{deepseekv3}), where a naive layer-wise partition leads to mismatched execution times across stages.
    The introduction of Multi-Token Prediction (MTP)~\cite{eagle, deepseekv3} further exacerbates this imbalance.
    Existing MTP implementations, primarily based on EAGLE~\cite{eagle}, consist of three modules---Embedding layer, MTP layer, and LM Head---where the Embedding and LM Head typically reuse weights from the base model.
    Naively integrating MTP with PP produces several issues: First, PP introduces extra inter-stage communication by separating the Embedding layer (in Stage 0) from the MTP layer and LM Head (both typically in Stage $N-1$).
    Second, the mutual dependency between the MTP and base model execution flows induces pipeline bubbles.
    Third, it can lead to hardware idling; since the MTP model is architecturally limited to a maximum of three stages, any PP size exceeding three leaves certain machines unable to participate in MTP computation~\cite{}.
    
    \item \textbf{Phase-Interference Imbalance:} LLM inference is split into Prefill and Decode phases with distinct computational characteristics~\cite{splitwise, distserve}, where significant execution time variances easily lead to pipeline bubbles across batches~\cite{dejavu}.
    While disaggregated Prefill-Decode (PD) architectures~\cite{splitwise, distserve, dejavu, mooncake} mitigate cross-phase interference, the accumulation of KV cache in the Prefill stage still induces pipeline bubbles.
    To maximize bandwidth utilization, current implementations often defer KV cache transmission until all Prefill layers have completed.
    In a PP scenario, this forces each stage to wait for all subsequent stages to finish before transmitting and freeing its local KV cache.
    Under bursty high-concurrency traffic, initial micro-batches may saturate resources, forcing subsequent requests to wait until the final stage completes, resulting in substantial pipeline bubbles.
    Furthermore, PP introduces \textbf{KV cache sharding across stages}: each Prefill stage generates only a layer-wise KV cache slice, and each Decode stage only necessitates the retrieval of the KV cache slice corresponding to its assigned layers.
    This transforms simple point-to-point KV cache transfers into complex stage-to-stage transmission, posing a significant management challenge.
    
    \item \textbf{Request-Variability Imbalance:} In online inference, the extreme variance in request lengths naturally leads to highly inconsistent micro-batch execution times~\cite{}.
    To mitigate these workloads, existing schedulers (e.g., Sarathi-Serve~\cite{sarathi}) employ chunked prefill to mitigate this by chunking long requests.
    However, such token-budget-based methods assume a linear relationship between execution time and token count, overlooking the quadratic complexity of attention mechanisms~\cite{}.
    As sequence length increases, quadratic attention computation becomes dominant.
    Since subsequent chunks must attend to the KV cache of all preceding chunks, the computational overhead grows progressively, causing token-based scheduling to suffer from severe pipeline bubbles when handling long-context requests~\cite{}.
\end{itemize}

To address these imbalance challenges, we propose \sys, a pipeline-balanced high-performance inference system featuring a synergistic co-design of pipeline parallelism with other inference techniques.
To resolve \textbf{model-partitioning imbalance}, we integrate PP with MTP by introducing \textbf{Centralized MTP Processing} and \textbf{Overhead-Aware Partitioning}, which consolidates MTP computation into the final stage and balances stage-wise workloads through execution cost modeling.
To handle \textbf{phase-interference imbalance}, we unify PP with PD disaggregation by establishing connections based on PP stages to facilitate \textbf{Sliced KV Cache Management} and \textbf{Stage-wise KV Transmission}, effectively preventing accumulation of KV cache in the Prefill stage.
Finally, to manage \textbf{request-variability imbalance}, we design a \textbf{Computation-Aware Scheduling} algorithm that balances micro-batch workloads by explicitly modeling their execution time rather than relying solely on token counts.

Preliminary evaluations on a 32-NPU (4-node) Ascend 910C cluster running the 671B-parameter DeepSeek-R1 model demonstrate \sys's superior performance.
Under strict Service Level Objectives (SLOs), \sys boosts end-to-end throughput by up to 90.8\% compared to the open-source vLLM-Ascend standard, and outperforms the highly optimized OmniInfer industrial baseline by 9.47\%.
Crucially, it strictly bounds pipeline bubbles to under 5\% while maintaining zero accuracy degradation.

Our main contributions are as follows:
\begin{itemize}
    \item We systematically characterize three types of load imbalance in PP-based inference: \textbf{model-partitioning}, \textbf{phase-interference}, and \textbf{request-variability} imbalances.
    \item We propose \sys, an LLM inference system that achieves holistic balance through a synergistic co-design of pipeline parallelism with other inference techniques and a computation-aware scheduler.
    \item We perform a preliminary evaluation on production-scale models (e.g., DeepSeek-R1), demonstrating that \sys significantly improves throughput and hardware efficiency in cross-node scenarios.
\end{itemize}

\section{Motivation}
\label{sec:moti}

In this section, we analyze why Pipeline Parallelism is the most viable strategy for deploying massive models like DeepSeek-R1 in cross-node scenarios and expose the fundamental causes of the pipeline bubble.

\subsection{The Case for Pipeline Parallelism}

The evolution of Large Language Models has demonstrated the power of scaling laws: increasing model parameters enhances foundational capabilities, while extending inference-time computation improves complex reasoning.
However, the massive model size and the memory footprint of KV cache for long contexts exert immense pressure on GPU memory, often exceeding the capacity of a single computing node.
For instance, a DeepSeek-R1 model with 671B parameters surpasses the 640GB memory limit of a standard H800 node (8 $\times$ 80GB).
Consequently, multi-node deployment has become indispensable.

Among existing parallelism strategies, Data Parallelism (DP) scales throughput by replicating models but fails to address memory constraints as it does not partition the model.
Context Parallelism (CP), such as Ring Attention~\cite{ringattention}, handles long sequences by partitioning context but remains ineffective against massive weights and incurs high communication overhead by passing KV cache layer by layer.
Tensor Parallelism (TP)~\cite{megatron} achieves intra-layer parallelism but requires heavy All-Reduce communication, making it unsuitable for inter-node scenarios with limited bandwidth.
Moreover, for ``thin and tall'' models with smaller hidden sizes, excessive TP partitions matrices too finely, leading to degraded computation efficiency and cache hit rates.
For MoE models like DeepSeek-R1, Expert Parallelism (EP)~\cite{deepseekv3} is effective but suffers from high All-to-All communication costs and potential bubbles due to expert load imbalance.

Compared to these strategies, Pipeline Parallelism only transmits a small volume of activations between stages, resulting in significantly lower communication traffic (as shown in Table~\ref{tab:parallel_comm}).
This makes PP a robust candidate for inter-node deployment.
While industry systems often employ engineering tricks like double-batching to hide communication costs, the inherent dependency between computation and communication for a single request means that inference latency remains bottlenecked by slow inter-node links.
Therefore, while PP offers a path to low-bandwidth multi-node inference, the notorious pipeline bubble issue remains the primary obstacle that must be addressed.

\subsection{Sources of Pipeline Imbalance}

While Pipeline Parallelism offers significant potential for cross-node deployment of large-scale models, its performance is often hindered by the notorious pipeline bubbles.
Achieving a balanced pipeline requires both near-identical processing times across stages and consistent execution durations for each micro-batch.
In real-world inference systems, a trivial PP implementation often introduces severe load imbalance.

We categorize the root causes of this imbalance into three dimensions.

\subsubsection{Structural Imbalance from Heterogeneous Architectures}

Traditional pipeline balancing assumes homogeneous workloads across layers.
However, modern models like DeepSeek-R1 introduce heterogeneous structures, such as initial Dense layers followed by Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) layers, which invalidate simple layer-wise partitioning.
A more significant challenge arises from Multi-Token Prediction (MTP).
In the computation flow, MTP modules are invoked multiple times after the main model, necessitating a weighted consideration of their overhead.
Structurally, MTP modules share the Embedding layer (at Stage 0) and the Output Head (at Stage $N-1$) with the original model for weight consistency.
This spatial dispersion forces intermediate results to ``round-trip'' between the first and last nodes, introducing not only extra communication but also structural bubbles as intermediate nodes remain idle during these transfers.

\subsubsection{Operational Imbalance in Disaggregated Prefill-Decode Phases}

The stark difference in computational characteristics between the Prefill and Decode phases is a primary driver of imbalance.
Although Prefill-Decode disaggregation has become the standard to mitigate interference, it introduces new bubbles in PP scenarios due to KV cache transfers.
Current systems typically aggregate KV cache for all layers and transmit them after the entire Prefill stage is completed to maintain high bandwidth utilization and avoid network congestion.
In a PP pipeline, this ``all-at-once'' transfer causes KV cache to accumulate on Prefill nodes.
If this accumulation saturates memory resources, Prefill nodes are forced to stall and cannot process new micro-batches until the downstream stages receive the cached data.
Furthermore, PP fragments the KV cache across stages, complicating the stage-to-stage management and transfer of these data slices.

\subsubsection{Computational Imbalance from Dynamic Sequence Lengths}

The massive variance in request lengths in online inference makes it difficult to align micro-batch execution times.
While schedulers like Sarathi-Serve employ Chunked Prefill to balance workloads by limiting token counts, this approach assumes a linear relationship between token count and execution time.
This assumption fails to account for the $O(S^2)$ complexity of Attention mechanisms and the cumulative impact of KV cache from previous chunks.
Our experiments indicate that for a 128k sequence, the execution time of the final 8k chunk can be significantly longer than the first.
Existing solutions using dynamic programming~\cite{terapipe} incur high computational complexity ($O(mnl^2)$) and are difficult to apply to varying request lengths, while FLOPs-based empirical formulas often fail to capture the actual execution time variations introduced by operator-level optimizations like FlashAttention~\cite{flashattention}.

\section{Research Methodology}

In this section, we present the detailed system design of \sys.
We first provide an overview of the system architecture, followed by an in-depth description of the three core techniques developed to address the multi-dimensional load imbalance in pipeline-parallel LLM inference.

\subsection{System Overview}

Figure~\ref{fig:overview} illustrates the overall architecture of \sys.
\sys adopts a disaggregated Prefill-Decode architecture, partitioning the compute cluster into separate Prefill and Decode pools, each potentially configured with different pipeline parallelism strategies.
Both Prefill and Decode instances are equipped with MTP modules: the Prefill instance's MTP module is invoked once per request to generate the MTP KV cache, while the Decode instance's MTP module is invoked multiple times per generation step to produce multiple candidate tokens.
Between Prefill and Decode nodes, \sys establishes stage-to-stage KV cache transfer connections, managed by dedicated KV Connectors that enable fine-grained, on-demand KV cache transmission.

    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{pics/overview.pdf}
    \caption{System Overview of \sys. The architecture features disaggregated Prefill-Decode clusters with stage-wise KV cache management and computation-aware scheduling.}
    \label{fig:overview}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Centralized MTP Processing and Overhead-Aware Partitioning}

To address the model-partitioning imbalance, we propose a two-pronged approach: Centralized MTP Processing and Overhead-Aware Partitioning.

\subsubsection{Centralized MTP Processing}

As analyzed in Section~\ref{sec:moti}, naively integrating MTP with PP introduces extra inter-stage communication and leaves intermediate stages idle during MTP computation.
The root cause is that PP's layer-wise partitioning disperses MTP components across different stages.
To eliminate this structural conflict, we consolidate all MTP modules and their execution into the final pipeline stage (Stage $N-1$) by redundantly loading the Embedding layer weights.

This design offers several advantages:
(1) It completely eliminates the ``round-trip'' communication between Stage 0 and Stage $N-1$ during MTP execution;
(2) It allows all stages to participate in the base model computation without idle time;
(3) The redundant Embedding layer has minimal memory overhead compared to the full model weights.

\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.9\textwidth]{pics/mtp-solu2.pdf}
    \caption{Centralized MTP Processing Design. All MTP computation is consolidated into Stage $N-1$, eliminating cross-stage communication.}
    \label{fig:mtp-solution}
\end{figure}

\subsubsection{Overhead-Aware Partitioning}

While the centralized MTP design eliminates communication overhead, it makes Stage $N-1$ a computation hotspot, as it must handle both the base model layers and the multi-round MTP computation.
To resolve this, we propose an Overhead-Aware Balanced Partitioning algorithm.

Unlike existing PP partitioning methods that treat MTP as a single layer, our approach explicitly accounts for the multi-round invocation overhead of MTP modules.
We first profile the per-layer execution costs through offline benchmarking, then formulate the partition as a discrete optimization problem.
The key insight is that partition points can only be placed at residual connection boundaries to maintain model structural integrity.

The optimization objective is to minimize the total execution time for $N$ micro-batches:
\begin{equation}
T = \sum_{i=1}^{P} T_{stage_i} + (N-1) \cdot T_{max}
\end{equation}
where $P$ is the number of pipeline stages and $T_{max}$ is the bottleneck stage latency.
Since $\sum_{i=1}^{P} T_{stage_i}$ is fixed for a given model, the objective reduces to minimizing $T_{max}$, which can be efficiently solved using binary search combined with greedy assignment.

After balanced partitioning, Stage $N-1$ is assigned fewer base model layers to compensate for the MTP overhead, which also naturally offsets the memory overhead of the redundantly loaded Embedding layer.

\subsection{Sliced KV Cache Management and Stage-wise Transmission}

To address the phase-interference imbalance, we propose a sliced KV cache management strategy with stage-wise transmission.

\subsubsection{Sliced KV Cache Management}

In PP-based PD disaggregation, each Prefill stage generates only a layer-wise slice of the complete KV cache, and each Decode stage requires only the slice corresponding to its assigned layers.
This transforms the simple point-to-point KV cache transfer into a complex many-to-many routing problem.

\sys addresses this by assigning a dedicated KV Cache Connector to each pipeline stage rather than using a monolithic connector for the entire cluster.
Each stage's connector independently manages its KV cache slice, tracking memory allocation, transfer status, and garbage collection.

\subsubsection{Stage-wise On-Demand Transmission}

The conventional approach of deferring KV cache transmission until all Prefill stages complete causes memory accumulation and potential pipeline stalls.
\sys leverages the strictly uni-directional data dependency of KV cache generation to enable fine-grained, on-demand transmission.

The transmission follows a pull-based model:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item During initialization, each Decode stage's KV Connector computes which Prefill stages it needs to request KV cache from, based on the layer assignment and PP configuration.
    \item When a Decode stage is ready to receive KV cache, it simultaneously sends pull requests to all connected Prefill stages.
    \item Each Prefill stage, upon completing its computation for a request, can immediately transfer and free its local KV cache slice without waiting for downstream stages.
\end{enumerate}

This design effectively eliminates Prefill memory exhaustion and pipeline stalls, while the stage-level transmission granularity balances bandwidth utilization and communication overhead.

\subsection{Computation-Aware Scheduling}

To address the request-variability imbalance, we replace static token-budgeting with fine-grained time-budgeting.

\subsubsection{Quadratic Cost Model}

We model the execution time of a micro-batch as a quadratic function of chunk length ($x$) and historical context length ($y$):
\begin{equation}
T_{x,y} = a \cdot x^2 + b \cdot x \cdot y + c \cdot x + d \cdot y + e
\end{equation}

This formulation captures the $O(S^2)$ complexity of attention mechanisms, where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $x^2$ term captures the self-attention within the current chunk
    \item $x \cdot y$ term captures the cross-attention between current chunk and historical context
    \item Linear terms capture the feed-forward and other linear operations
\end{itemize}

The coefficients $(a, b, c, d, e)$ are initialized through offline profiling with a few sample points and dynamically updated during runtime using Recursive Least Squares (RLS) based on actual recorded latencies.

\subsubsection{Time-Budget Scheduling}

The maximum time budget is empirically set to the execution time of a zero-context request that precisely saturates the hardware resources, mathematically guaranteeing an OOM-free runtime.

\begin{algorithm}[h]
\caption{Computation-Aware Time-Budget Scheduling}
\label{alg:scheduling}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\STATE Initialize coefficients $(a,b,c,d,e)$ via offline profiling
\STATE Set maximum budget $T_{budget}$
\STATE \textbf{loop} for each scheduling step
\STATE \quad $\mathcal{M} \leftarrow \emptyset$, $B \leftarrow T_{budget}$
\STATE \quad \textbf{while} Request Queue not empty \textbf{and} $B > 0$ \textbf{do}
\STATE \quad \quad Pop request $r$ with context length $y_r$
\STATE \quad \quad Calculate estimated time $T_{est}$ for full chunk
\STATE \quad \quad \textbf{if} $T_{est} \le B$ \textbf{then}
\STATE \quad \quad \quad Allocate full chunk, $\mathcal{M} \leftarrow \mathcal{M} \cup \{r\}$
\STATE \quad \quad \quad $B \leftarrow B - T_{est}$
\STATE \quad \quad \textbf{else}
\STATE \quad \quad \quad Reverse-calculate maximum chunk size $x_r$ s.t. $T_{x_r, y_r} \le B$
\STATE \quad \quad \quad Allocate chunk of size $x_r$, update request in queue
\STATE \quad \quad \quad $B \leftarrow 0$
\STATE \quad \quad \textbf{end if}
\STATE \quad \textbf{end while}
\STATE \quad Execute micro-batch $\mathcal{M}$ and record $T_{actual}$
\STATE \quad Update coefficients via RLS using $(x, y, T_{actual})$
\STATE \textbf{end loop}
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

Algorithm~\ref{alg:scheduling} presents the detailed scheduling procedure.
By explicitly modeling execution time rather than relying on token counts, \sys achieves balanced micro-batch execution times even under highly variable request lengths, effectively eliminating request-variability induced pipeline bubbles.

\section{Preliminary Evaluation}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}

\textbf{Hardware and Model.}
We implemented \sys on top of the vLLM framework (v0.6.3).
The evaluation was conducted on a 32-NPU (4-node) Ascend 910C cluster, with each node containing 8 NPUs interconnected via HCCS.
We deployed the DeepSeek-R1 (671B) model, which features a Mixture-of-Experts architecture with 61 active experts per layer and supports Multi-Token Prediction for speculative decoding.

\textbf{Baselines.}
We compared \sys against two representative baselines:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{vLLM-Ascend:} The standard open-source inference engine for Ascend NPUs, employing TP-only parallelism without PP or MTP support.
    \item \textbf{OmniInfer:} A state-of-the-art industrial solution that implements MTP and PD disaggregation but without PP integration.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Workloads.}
We evaluated under two workload configurations:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Short-context (2K):} Request lengths uniformly sampled from [512, 2048] tokens.
    \item \textbf{Long-context (32K):} Request lengths uniformly sampled from [16K, 32K] tokens.
\end{itemize}
Each configuration includes both fixed-length and variable-length request patterns to assess scheduling robustness.

\textbf{Metrics.}
We measure: (1) \textbf{Throughput} (requests/second) under strict SLA constraints (TTFT $<$ 2s, TPOT $<$ 100ms); (2) \textbf{Pipeline Bubble Ratio} defined as idle time / total execution time; (3) \textbf{Accuracy} on the HumanEval benchmark to verify correctness.

\section{Future Work}

While the current architecture of \textsc{\sys} significantly resolves the load imbalance issues in pipeline-parallel LLM inference, deploying such large-scale distributed systems in production cloud environments introduces reliability and dynamic scaling challenges. Our future work will focus on the following directions:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Automated Fault Tolerance for Pipeline Parallelism:} In a cross-node PP setup, the failure of a single node (or NPU) can stall the entire pipeline, leading to catastrophic inference failures. We plan to design a lightweight, state-aware recovery mechanism. By utilizing the stage-wise KV Cache Connectors introduced in \textsc{\sys}, we can periodically checkpoint metadata and KV states asynchronously, enabling rapid recreation of failed stages without recomputing the entire prompt from scratch.
    
    \item \textbf{Elastic Pipeline Reconfiguration:} Currently, the overhead-aware partitioning is calculated statically during initialization. Real-world inference traffic fluctuates drastically, shifting the bottleneck dynamically between Prefill and Decode phases. We will explore an elastic scaling scheduler that can dynamically adjust the number of pipeline stages or re-partition layers on the fly (via hot-swapping weights) to adapt to real-time traffic characteristics, maximizing hardware utilization.
    
    \item \textbf{Multi-Tenant MoE Routing Optimization:} As models like DeepSeek-R1 rely heavily on Mixture-of-Experts (MoE), expert load imbalance remains a secondary bottleneck. We plan to integrate cross-batch and multi-tenant expert routing strategies into \textsc{\sys}'s computation-aware scheduler. By co-scheduling requests with overlapping expert affinities, we aim to reduce expert-to-expert communication overhead across pipeline stages.
\end{itemize}

By addressing these systems-level challenges, we aim to evolve \textsc{\sys} into a highly resilient, fully autonomous inference infrastructure capable of sustaining the next generation of trillion-parameter models.
