\begin{center}
    \Large \textbf{Abstract}
\end{center}
\vspace{0.5cm}
\setstretch{1.15}

The unprecedented growth in both parameter scale and context window size of Large Language Models (LLMs) has rendered multi-node distributed inference an essential requirement for modern deployment.
However, prevailing parallelism strategies employed in inference scenarios---including Tensor Parallelism (TP), Expert Parallelism (EP), and Context Parallelism (CP)---suffer from significant inefficiencies attributable to substantial inter-node communication overhead.
Although Pipeline Parallelism (PP) presents a promising approach to mitigate these communication bottlenecks, it frequently encounters severe load imbalances---manifesting as pipeline bubbles---stemming from the inherent heterogeneities and dynamic workloads characteristic of LLM inference.
In this work, we systematically characterize three primary load imbalance challenges in PP-based inference: \modelimb, \phaseimb, and \requestimb.

To address these challenges, we present \sys, a novel pipeline-balanced inference system designed to bridge the efficiency gap through a synergistic co-design of pipeline parallelism with state-of-the-art inference techniques.
Specifically, \sys introduces: (1) \centralmtp and \overheadpartition to resolve \modelimb; (2) \slicedkv and \stagekv to eliminate \phaseimb; and (3) \timesched leveraging a quadratic cost model to handle \requestimb.

Preliminary evaluations on a \clusterconfig with DeepSeek-R1 (671B) demonstrate that \sys achieves up to \vllmgain end-to-end throughput improvement over state-of-the-art baselines such as vLLM-Ascend under identical SLO constraints, while strictly bounding pipeline bubbles under \bubblecap with zero accuracy degradation.

\vspace{1cm}
\noindent \textbf{Keywords:} Large Language Models (LLMs), Pipeline Parallelism (PP), Inference System.

\section{Introduction}

Large Language Models (LLMs) have emerged as the cornerstone of modern artificial intelligence~\cite{survey}.
The industry is witnessing a substantial capital reallocation, with compute budgets definitively shifting from model training to deployment, thereby rendering inference efficiency the new critical bottleneck~\cite{splitwise}.
This rapid evolution has been characterized by a massive expansion in both parameter scale (e.g., DeepSeek-R1 671B~\cite{deepseek-r1}, Kimi K2 1T~\cite{kimi2}) and context window sizes (e.g., Qwen3 1M tokens~\cite{qwen3}).

While unlocking unprecedented capabilities, this expansion imposes prohibitive memory constraints on the underlying serving infrastructure.
A single inference node (e.g., equipped with $8 \times$ H800 GPUs) often lacks sufficient High Bandwidth Memory (HBM) to host the full weights of massive models alongside the runtime KV cache under large batch sizes.
Consequently, multi-node distributed inference has become an imperative necessity for deployment, shifting the system bottleneck from memory capacity to inter-node communication bandwidth.

However, commonly employed parallelism strategies in inference scenarios---such as Tensor Parallelism (TP)~\cite{megatron}, Expert Parallelism (EP)~\cite{deepseekv3}, and Context Parallelism (CP)~\cite{ringattention}---suffer from severe inefficiencies due to substantial inter-node communication overhead~\cite{splitwise,distserve}.
For instance, TP relies heavily on layer-wise, high-bandwidth-consuming All-Reduce collectives, which induces severe latency degradation across nodes~\cite{alpa}.
% Furthermore, for ``deep and thin'' models, TP fragments the already narrow hidden dimensions too finely, leading to insufficient computation grain size and diminished hardware utilization.

To mitigate inter-node communication bottlenecks, Pipeline Parallelism (PP)~\cite{gpipe} emerges as an ideal candidate, as it requires only the transfer of a limited volume of hidden states between stages.
% Compared to TP, PP enables each layer to maintain a more complete computation shape, sustaining higher arithmetic intensity and cache locality in ``deep and thin'' models.
However, while PP is widely adopted in large-scale training, its application to inference scenarios remains challenging.
This is primarily because the inherent heterogeneities and dynamic workloads characteristic of LLM inference frequently trigger various forms of load imbalance, manifesting as pipeline bubbles~\cite{gpipe}.
We systematically categorize these imbalances into three primary dimensions:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{\modelimb:} Many contemporary LLMs exhibit heterogeneous workloads across layers (e.g., DeepSeek-R1 integrates both dense and MoE layers~\cite{deepseek-r1,deepseekv3}), where naive layer-wise partitioning leads to mismatched execution times across stages.
    The introduction of \mtp~\cite{eagle,deepseekv3} further exacerbates this imbalance.
    Existing MTP implementations, primarily based on EAGLE~\cite{eagle}, comprise three modules---Embedding layer, MTP layer, and LM Head---where the Embedding layer and LM Head typically reuse weights from the base model.
    Naively integrating \mtp with PP produces several issues: First, PP introduces additional inter-stage communication by separating the Embedding layer (in Stage 0) from the MTP layer and LM Head (both typically in Stage $N-1$).
    Second, the mutual dependency between \mtp and the base-model execution flow induces pipeline bubbles.
    Third, it can lead to hardware idling; since the MTP model is architecturally limited to a maximum of three stages, any PP configuration exceeding three stages leaves certain machines unable to participate in MTP computation.
    
    \item \textbf{\phaseimb:} LLM inference comprises two distinct phases with fundamentally different computational characteristics---\prefillterm and \decodeterm~\cite{splitwise,distserve}, where significant execution time variances readily lead to pipeline bubbles across batches~\cite{dejavu}.
    While Prefill-Decode (PD) disaggregation architectures~\cite{splitwise, distserve, dejavu, mooncake} mitigate cross-phase interference, the accumulation of KV cache in the Prefill stage still induces pipeline bubbles.
    To maximize bandwidth utilization, current implementations often defer KV cache transmission until all Prefill layers have completed.%这句话有点突兀
    In a PP scenario, this forces each stage to wait for all subsequent stages to finish before transmitting and freeing its local KV cache.
    Under bursty high-concurrency traffic, one micro-batch may saturate resources, forcing subsequent requests to wait until the final stage completes, thereby resulting in the whole pipeline stall.
    Furthermore, PP introduces \textbf{KV cache sharding across stages}: each Prefill stage generates only a layer-wise KV cache slice, and each Decode stage requires only the retrieval of the KV cache slice corresponding to its assigned layers.
    This transformation of simple point-to-point KV cache transfers into complex stage-to-stage transmission poses a significant management challenge.
    
    \item \textbf{\requestimb:} In online inference, the extreme variance in request lengths naturally leads to highly inconsistent micro-batch execution times~\cite{sarathi}, which is a non-negligible source of pipeline bubbles.
    To mitigate these challenges, existing schedulers (e.g., Sarathi-Serve~\cite{sarathi}) employ chunked prefill by partitioning long requests into smaller chunks.
    However, such token-budget-based methods assume a linear relationship between execution time and token count, fundamentally overlooking the quadratic complexity of attention mechanisms~\cite{attention-complexity}.
    As sequence length increases, quadratic attention computation becomes dominant.
    Since subsequent chunks must attend to the KV cache of all preceding chunks, the computational overhead grows progressively, causing token-based scheduling to suffer from severe pipeline bubbles when handling long-context requests~\cite{attention-complexity}.
\end{itemize}

To address these imbalance challenges, we propose \sys, a pipeline-balanced high-performance inference system featuring a synergistic co-design of pipeline parallelism with complementary inference techniques.
To resolve \textbf{\modelimb}, we integrate PP with \mtp by introducing \textbf{\centralmtp} and \textbf{\overheadpartition}, which consolidate MTP computation into the final stage and balance stage-wise workloads through execution-cost modeling.
To handle \textbf{\phaseimb}, we unify PP with PD disaggregation by establishing connections based on PP stages to facilitate \textbf{\slicedkv} and \textbf{\stagekv}, effectively preventing KV cache accumulation in the \prefillterm stage.
Finally, to manage \textbf{\requestimb}, we design a \textbf{\timesched} algorithm that balances micro-batch workloads by explicitly modeling their execution time rather than relying solely on token counts.

Preliminary evaluations on a \clusterconfig running the 671B-parameter DeepSeek-R1 model demonstrate \sys's superior performance.
Under strict Service Level Objectives (SLOs), \sys enhances end-to-end throughput by up to \vllmgain compared to the open-source vLLM-Ascend standard, and outperforms the highly optimized OmniInfer industrial baseline by \omnigain.
Crucially, it strictly bounds pipeline bubbles to under \bubblecap while maintaining zero accuracy degradation.

Our main contributions are as follows:
\begin{itemize}
    \item We systematically characterize three types of load imbalance in PP-based inference: \textbf{\modelimb}, \textbf{\phaseimb}, and \textbf{\requestimb}.
    \item We propose \sys, an LLM inference system that achieves holistic balance through a synergistic co-design of pipeline parallelism with complementary inference techniques and a computation-aware scheduler.
    \item We perform a preliminary evaluation on production-scale models (e.g., DeepSeek-R1), demonstrating that \sys significantly improves throughput and hardware efficiency in cross-node scenarios.
\end{itemize}

\section{Motivation}
\label{sec:moti}

In this section, we analyze why Pipeline Parallelism represents the most viable strategy for deploying massive models such as DeepSeek-R1 in cross-node scenarios and expose the fundamental causes of pipeline bubbles.

\subsection{The Opportunity for Pipeline Parallelism}

The evolution of Large Language Models has demonstrated the power of scaling laws: increasing model parameters enhances foundational capabilities, while extending inference-time computation improves complex reasoning.
However, the massive model size and the memory footprint of KV cache for long contexts exert immense pressure on GPU memory, frequently exceeding the capacity of a single computing node.
For instance, a DeepSeek-R1 model with 671B parameters surpasses the 640GB memory limit of a standard H800 node (8 $\times$ 80GB).
Consequently, multi-node deployment has become indispensable.

Among existing parallelism strategies, Data Parallelism (DP) scales throughput by replicating models but fails to address memory constraints, as it does not partition the model.
Context Parallelism (CP), such as Ring Attention~\cite{ringattention}, handles long sequences by partitioning context but remains ineffective against massive weights and incurs high communication overhead by passing KV cache layer by layer.
Tensor Parallelism (TP)~\cite{megatron} achieves intra-layer parallelism but requires heavy layer-wise All-Reduce communication, rendering it unsuitable for inter-node scenarios with limited bandwidth.
Moreover, for ``thin and tall'' models with smaller hidden sizes, excessive TP partitions matrices too finely, leading to degraded computation efficiency and cache hit rates.
For MoE models such as DeepSeek-R1, Expert Parallelism (EP)~\cite{deepseekv3} is effective but suffers from high layer-wise All-to-All communication costs and potential bubbles due to expert load imbalance.

Compared to these strategies, Pipeline Parallelism transmits only a small volume of activations between stages, resulting in significantly lower communication traffic (as shown in Table~\ref{tab:parallel_comm}).
% This renders PP a robust candidate for inter-node deployment.
While industry systems often employ engineering techniques such as double-batching to hide communication costs, the inherent dependency between computation and communication for a single request implies that inference latency remains bottlenecked by slow inter-node links.
Therefore, PP is a very promising optimization candidate because it fundamentally reduces cross-node communication overhead.

However, while PP offers a pathway to low-bandwidth multi-node inference, the pipeline imbalance bubble issue remains the primary obstacle that must be addressed.

\subsection{Sources of Pipeline Imbalance}

While Pipeline Parallelism offers significant potential for cross-node deployment of large-scale models, its performance is often hindered by pipeline bubbles.
Pipeline bubbles can be categorized into two types: \textbf{inherent bubbles} and \textbf{imbalance bubbles}, as shown in Figure~\ref{fig:2bubbetypes}.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.75\textwidth]{pics/2-bubble-types.pdf}
    \caption{Two pipeline bubble types: \textbf{inherent bubbles} and \textbf{imbalance bubbles}.}
    \label{fig:2bubbetypes}
\end{figure}

Inherent bubbles arise from the intrinsic data dependencies during pipeline warm-up and cool-down phases.
During warm-up, early stages process the first micro-batch while later stages remain idle; during cool-down, later stages process the final micro-batch while earlier stages have completed.
These inherent bubbles can be amortized and safely ignored over long generation periods when the number of micro-batches significantly exceeds the number of pipeline stages ($N \gg P$)~\cite{gpipe}.

In contrast, \textbf{imbalance bubbles} persist throughout the entire pipeline execution, continuously degrading GPU utilization and must be systematically mitigated.
As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:imbalance}, these bubbles stem from three fundamental causes: (1) \textit{uneven stage partitioning}, where different stages exhibit mismatched execution times; (2) \textit{inconsistent micro-batch workloads}, where different micro-batches require varying processing times; and (3) \textit{data dependencies}, where stages must wait for data from other stages before proceeding.
In the context of modern LLM inference, we identify three specific types of imbalance bubbles arising from these fundamental causes: \textbf{\modelimb}, \textbf{\phaseimb}, and \textbf{\requestimb}.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{pics/imbalance-stage-batch.pdf}
    \caption{Three root causes of imbalance bubbles: uneven stage partitioning, inconsistent micro-batch workloads, and cross-stage data dependencies.}
    \label{fig:imbalance}
\end{figure}

\subsubsection{\modelimb}

Modern LLMs exhibit heterogeneous architectural characteristics that invalidate naive layer-wise partitioning strategies.

Contemporary LLMs integrate Dense and Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) FFNs~\cite{deepseekv3}, as well as Full and Linear Attention mechanisms across different layers.
Naive partitioning by layer count inevitably leads to severe workload imbalances across stages, as different layer types exhibit vastly different computational costs.

A more significant challenge arises from \mtp~\cite{eagle, deepseekv3}, as shown in Figure~\ref{fig:mtp-challenge}. 
\mtp is a training-inference co-designed speculative decoding paradigm that is becoming the new industry standard for high-throughput inference.
MTP modules deeply share weights with the base model's Embedding and LM Head.
In naive PP, placing the Embedding at Stage 0 and the LM Head at Stage $N-1$ forces intermediate activations to ``round-trip'' across the cluster, incurring additional inter-stage communication.
This mutual dependency also stalls intermediate stages during MTP computation, introducing structural imbalance bubbles as these stages remain idle.

\subsubsection{\phaseimb}

LLM inference comprises two distinct phases with fundamentally different computational characteristics: the Prefill phase, which processes the prompt in parallel to generate the first output token, exhibiting compute-bound behavior; and the Decode phase, which iteratively generates subsequent tokens one by one, exhibiting memory-bound behavior~\cite{splitwise, distserve}.
This inherent dichotomy creates interference when both phases are co-located.

A single \prefillterm step often takes substantially longer than a \decodeterm step, causing prefill-dominant micro-batches to run significantly slower than decode-dominant batches, which drastically degrades Time Between Tokens (TBT).
This drastic time discrepancy creates highly variable micro-batch execution times, thereby generating imbalance bubbles.

While disaggregated Prefill-Decode (PD) architectures~\cite{splitwise, distserve, dejavu, mooncake} have become the standard approach to mitigate cross-phase interference, they introduce new challenges in PP scenarios.

Existing systems widely employ bulk transmission, deferring KV cache transfer until the entire Prefill phase completes to maximize bandwidth utilization.
However, under Pipeline Parallelism, this mechanism forces early stages (e.g., Stage 0) to passively wait for all subsequent stages to finish before they can transmit and release their local KV cache.
As illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:kv-accumulation}, under bursty traffic conditions, large micro-batches exhaust early-stage memory resources.
Since the KV cache is held until the end of the pipeline, subsequent micro-batches are starved of resources, forcing the whole pipeline to stall and creating massive bubbles.

Furthermore, PP inherently fragments the KV cache across stages, transforming traditional simple point-to-point communication into complex stage-to-stage routing.
Each Prefill stage generates only its assigned layers' KV cache slice, and each Decode stage requires only the slice corresponding to its assigned layers.
This many-to-many routing pattern poses significant management challenges and threatens system robustness.

\end{figure}

\subsubsection{\requestimb}

The substantial variance in request lengths makes it difficult to align micro-batch execution times, thereby introducing further pipeline bubbles.
Existing schedulers (e.g., Sarathi-Serve~\cite{sarathi}) employ Chunked Prefill to mitigate this by partitioning long requests into smaller chunks and limiting the total token count per micro-batch.
However, such token-budget-based methods assume a linear relationship between execution time and token count, fundamentally overlooking the $O(S^2)$ quadratic complexity of attention mechanisms.

As sequence length increases, quadratic attention computation becomes dominant.
Since subsequent chunks must attend to the KV cache of all preceding chunks, the computational overhead grows progressively~\cite{attention-complexity}.
This progressive increase in chunk execution time causes token-based scheduling to suffer from severe pipeline bubbles when handling long-context requests, as shown in Figure~\ref{fig:sarathi-bubble}.

Existing solutions face significant limitations.
Dynamic programming approaches~\cite{terapipe} can theoretically optimize chunk sizes for balanced execution times, but they incur high computational complexity ($O(mnl^2)$) and are difficult to apply to varying request lengths in real-time inference scenarios.
FLOPs-based empirical formulas attempt to estimate execution time through computational cost modeling, but they account only for theoretical computation while overlooking memory access and communication overhead.
Furthermore, these formulas rely on theoretical FLOPs values, which may deviate significantly from actual execution costs due to varying operator implementations, such as FlashAttention~\cite{flashattention}.
These optimizations can significantly alter the relationship between theoretical FLOPs and actual wall-clock time, rendering simple formulas inaccurate.

\section{Research Methodology}

In this section, we present the preliminary system design of \sys.
We first provide an overview of the system architecture, followed by an in-depth description of the three core techniques developed to address the multi-dimensional load imbalance in pipeline-parallel LLM inference.

\subsection{System Overview}

Figure~\ref{fig:overview} illustrates the overall architecture of \sys.
\sys adopts a disaggregated Prefill-Decode architecture, partitioning the compute cluster into separate Prefill and Decode pools, each potentially configured with different pipeline parallelism strategies.
Both Prefill and Decode instances are equipped with MTP modules: the Prefill instance's MTP module is invoked once per request to generate the MTP KV cache, while the Decode instance's MTP module is invoked multiple times per generation step to produce multiple candidate tokens.
Between Prefill and Decode nodes, \sys establishes stage-to-stage KV cache transfer connections, managed by dedicated KV Connectors that enable fine-grained, on-demand KV cache transmission.

\subsection{\centralmtp and \overheadpartition}

To address the model-partitioning imbalance, we propose a two-pronged approach: Centralized MTP Processing and Overhead-Aware Stage Partitioning.

\subsubsection{Centralized MTP Processing}

As analyzed in Section~\ref{sec:moti}, naively integrating MTP with PP introduces extra inter-stage communication and leaves intermediate stages idle during MTP computation.
The root cause is that PP's layer-wise partitioning disperses MTP components across different stages.
To eliminate this structural conflict, we consolidate all MTP modules and their execution into the final pipeline stage (Stage $N-1$) by redundantly loading the Embedding layer weights, as illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:mtp-solution}.

This design offers several advantages:
(1) It completely eliminates the ``round-trip'' communication between Stage 0 and Stage $N-1$ during MTP execution;
(2) It allows all stages to participate in the base model computation without idle time.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{pics/mtp-solu2.pdf}
    \caption{Centralized MTP processing. All MTP computation is consolidated into Stage $N-1$, eliminating cross-stage MTP communication.}
    \label{fig:mtp-solution}
\end{figure}

\subsubsection{Overhead-Aware Stage Partitioning}

While the centralized MTP design eliminates communication overhead, it makes Stage $N-1$ a computation hotspot, as it must handle both the base model layers and the multi-round MTP computation.
To resolve this, we propose an Overhead-Aware Balanced Partitioning algorithm.

Unlike existing PP partitioning methods that treat MTP as a single layer, our approach explicitly accounts for the multi-round invocation overhead of MTP modules.
We first profile the per-layer execution costs through offline benchmarking, then formulate the partition as a discrete optimization problem.
The key insight is that partition points can only be placed at residual connection boundaries to maintain model structural integrity and minimize communication overhead.

The optimization objective is to minimize the total execution time for $N$ micro-batches:
\begin{equation}
T = \sum_{i=1}^{P} T_{stage_i} + (N-1) \cdot T_{max}
\end{equation}
where $P$ is the number of pipeline stages and $T_{max}$ is the bottleneck stage latency.
Since $\sum_{i=1}^{P} T_{stage_i}$ is fixed for a given model, the objective reduces to minimizing $T_{max}$, which can be efficiently solved using binary search combined with greedy assignment.

After balanced partitioning, Stage $N-1$ is assigned fewer base model layers to compensate for the MTP overhead, which also naturally offsets the memory overhead of the redundantly loaded Embedding layer.

\subsection{\slicedkv and \stagekv}

To address the phase-interference imbalance, we propose a sliced KV cache management strategy with stage-wise KV transmission.

\subsubsection{Sliced KV Cache Management}

In PP-based PD disaggregation, each Prefill stage generates only a layer-wise slice of the complete KV cache, and each Decode stage requires only the slice corresponding to its assigned layers.
This transforms the simple point-to-point KV cache transfer into a complex many-to-many routing problem.

\sys addresses this by assigning a dedicated KV Cache Connector to each pipeline stage rather than using a monolithic connector for the entire cluster.
Each stage's connector independently manages its KV cache slice, including tracking memory allocation, transfer status, and garbage collection.

\subsubsection{Stage-wise KV Transmission}

The conventional approach of deferring KV cache transmission until all Prefill stages complete causes memory accumulation and potential pipeline stalls.
\sys leverages the strictly uni-directional data dependency of KV cache generation to enable stage-wise KV transmission, as shown in Figure~\ref{fig:stage-wise-kv}.

%暂时到这里
The transmission follows a pull-based model:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item During initialization, each Decode stage's KV Connector computes which Prefill stages it needs to request KV cache from, based on the layer assignment and PP configuration.
    \item When a Decode stage is ready to receive KV cache, it simultaneously sends pull requests to all connected Prefill stages.
    \item Each Prefill stage, upon completing its computation for a micro-batch, can immediately respond to KV cache pull requests from Decode stages, transferring and freeing its local KV cache slice without waiting for downstream stages.
\end{enumerate}

This design effectively eliminates Prefill memory exhaustion and pipeline stalls, while the stage-level transmission granularity balances bandwidth utilization and communication overhead.

\subsection{\timesched}

To address the request-variability imbalance, we replace static token-budgeting with fine-grained time-budgeting, which accounts for the quadratic attention complexity of LLMs. We call this approach Computation-Aware Scheduling.

\subsubsection{Quadratic Polynomial Cost Model}

We model the execution time of a micro-batch as a quadratic function of chunk length ($x$) and historical context length ($y$):
\begin{equation}
T_{x,y} = a \cdot x^2 + b \cdot x \cdot y + c \cdot x + d \cdot y + e
\end{equation}

This formulation captures the $O(S^2)$ complexity of attention mechanisms, where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $x^2$ term captures the quadratic self-attention within the current chunk
    \item $x \cdot y$ term captures the cross-attention between current chunk and historical context and softmax overhead
    \item $x$ term captures linear operations within the current chunk (e.g., linear projection, element-wise activation)
    \item $y$ term captures linear operations on historical context (e.g., MLA projection, linear memory access)
    \item Constant term captures fixed overhead (e.g., kernel launch, operator dispatch)
\end{itemize}

The coefficients $(a, b, c, d, e)$ are initialized through offline profiling with a few sample points and can be dynamically updated during runtime using Recursive Least Squares (RLS) based on actual recorded latencies~\cite{haykin}.
%RLS给一个citation

\subsubsection{Time-Budget Scheduling Algorithm}

Based on the quadratic polynomial cost model, we propose a time-budget scheduling algorithm that explicitly controls micro-batch execution time to achieve balanced workloads across the pipeline.
Unlike traditional token-budget approaches that assume linear time-token relationships, our method directly constrains execution time, naturally accounting for the quadratic attention complexity.

\begin{algorithm}[htbp]
\caption{Computation-Aware Scheduling with Time-Budget Constraint}
\label{alg:scheduling}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\State \textbf{Initialize} coefficients $\Theta = (a, b, c, d, e)$ via offline profiling
\State Set maximum budget $T_{budget}$
\Loop \text{ for each scheduling step}
    \State $\mathcal{M} \leftarrow \emptyset$
    \State $B \leftarrow T_{budget}$
    \While{Request Queue is not empty \textbf{and} $B > 0$}
        \State Pop request $r_i$ with remaining length $l_i$ and context length $y_i$
        \State $T_{est} \leftarrow a l_i^2 + b l_i y_i + c l_i + d y_i + e$
        \If{$T_{est} \le B$}
            \State $x_i \leftarrow l_i$
            \State $B \leftarrow B - T_{est}$
        \Else \Comment{Budget exceeded: reverse-calculate maximum allowable chunk size $x_i$}
            \State $x_i \leftarrow \left\lfloor \frac{-(b y_i + c) + \sqrt{(b y_i + c)^2 - 4a(d y_i + e - B)}}{2a} \right\rfloor$
            \State Update $r_i$'s remaining length to $l_i - x_i$ and push back to Queue
            \State $B \leftarrow 0$
        \EndIf
        \State $\mathcal{M} \leftarrow \mathcal{M} \cup \{ \text{chunk}(r_i, x_i) \}$
    \EndWhile
    \State Execute micro-batch $\mathcal{M}$ on Pipeline
    \State \textbf{[Optional]} Record actual execution latency $T_{actual}$
    \State \textbf{[Optional]} Update $\Theta$ using Recursive Least Squares (RLS) with $(\mathcal{M}, T_{actual})$
\EndLoop
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

Algorithm~\ref{alg:scheduling} presents the detailed scheduling procedure.
The algorithm maintains a time budget $T_{budget}$ for each micro-batch and iteratively allocates requests until the budget is exhausted.
For each request, it estimates the execution time using the quadratic model.
If the estimated time fits within the remaining budget, the full chunk is allocated; otherwise, the algorithm reverse-calculates the maximum chunk size that satisfies the budget constraint.
After execution, the actual latency is recorded to update the model coefficients via RLS, enabling continuous adaptation to workload characteristics.

The time budget hyperparameter $T_{budget}$ can be empirically set to the execution time of a zero-context request that precisely saturates hardware resources.
This heuristic ensures that each micro-batch maximally utilizes available memory and compute without exceeding capacity, mathematically guaranteeing an OOM-free runtime.

By explicitly modeling execution time rather than relying on token counts, \sys achieves balanced micro-batch execution times even under highly variable request lengths, effectively eliminating request-variability induced pipeline bubbles.

\section{Preliminary Evaluation}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}

\textbf{Hardware and Model.}
We implemented \sys on top of the OmniInfer framework~\cite{omniinfer}.
The evaluation was conducted on a 32-NPU (4-node) Ascend 910C cluster, with each node containing 8 NPUs interconnected via HCCL~\cite{hccl}.
We deployed the DeepSeek-R1 (671B) model~\cite{deepseek-r1}, which features a Mixture-of-Experts architecture and supports Multi-Token Prediction.

\textbf{Baselines.}
We compared \sys against two representative baselines:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{vLLM-Ascend~\cite{vllm, vllm-ascend}:} The standard open-source inference engine for Ascend NPUs.
    \item \textbf{OmniInfer~\cite{omniinfer}:} A state-of-the-art industrial solution that implements MTP and PD disaggregation but without PP integration.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Workloads.}
We evaluated under four workload configurations: Short-context Fixed (2K), Short-context Variable (2K), Long-context Fixed (32K), and Long-context Variable (32K).

\textbf{Metrics.}
We measure: (1) \textbf{Throughput} (requests/second) under strict latency SLO constraints; (2) \textbf{Pipeline Bubble Ratio} defined as idle time / total execution time; (3) \textbf{Accuracy} on the HumanEval benchmark to verify correctness.

\textbf{Research Questions.}
Based on the metrics, we aim to answer the following questions:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{RQ1 (Throughput):} How much does \sys improve end-to-end throughput compared to baselines under the same SLOs?
    \item \textbf{RQ2 (Pipeline Efficiency):} Does \sys effectively mitigate pipeline bubbles in highly variable-length request scenarios?
    \item \textbf{RQ3 (Robustness):} Can \sys guarantee zero degradation in model accuracy?
\end{itemize}

\section{Conclusion}

We systematically identified that naive pipeline parallelism in LLM inference fails due to multi-dimensional load imbalance: \modelimb, \phaseimb, and \requestimb.
We engineered \sys, the first pipeline-balanced high-performance LLM inference system.
Through a synergistic co-design of \centralmtp, \stagekv, and \timesched, \sys efficiently addresses all sources of imbalance bubbles.
Evaluated on DeepSeek-R1 (671B) over Ascend 910C clusters, \sys delivers up to \vllmgain throughput gains over open-source standards under the same SLO constraints, while maintaining zero accuracy degradation.
The system has been successfully deployed in production clusters, demonstrating its practical viability for large-scale LLM serving.

\section{Future Work}

While \sys significantly resolves load imbalance issues in pipeline-parallel LLM inference, there remains room for further improvement.
Our future work will focus on the following directions:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Automated Fault Tolerance for Pipeline Parallelism:} In a cross-node PP setup, the failure of a single node (or NPU) can stall the entire pipeline, leading to catastrophic inference failures.
    We plan to design a lightweight, state-aware recovery mechanism.
    By utilizing the stage-wise KV Cache Connectors introduced in \sys, we can periodically checkpoint metadata and KV states asynchronously, enabling rapid recreation of failed stages without recomputing the entire prompt from scratch.
    
    \item \textbf{Offline Inference Optimization:} In offline inference scenarios (e.g., the rollout phase in LLM reinforcement learning (RL) post-training~\cite{rl-posttraining}), throughput is the primary optimization objective.
    We plan to further optimize \sys for offline inference workloads, where batch size and sequence length can be more aggressively tuned to maximize hardware utilization without online latency constraints.
\end{itemize}

By addressing these challenges, we aim to evolve \sys into a highly resilient, fully autonomous inference infrastructure capable of sustaining the next generation of trillion-parameter models.
